Survey Unit Θ-9 had been operating in the northern Terran mountain zone for approximately nine of this world's rotational periods prior to the contact event. The survey mandate is standard for civilisations at this developmental stage: biological cataloguing, atmospheric sampling, assessment of cognitive and emotional development, and geopolitical monitoring. Terrans are of particular ongoing interest given the accelerated pace of their technological acquisition relative to their sociological maturity — a disparity that the ████████████████ has flagged as requiring continued elevated monitoring.

The mountain corridor in Sector 4 — a linear route system used by Terran ground vehicles — had been identified as an optimal observation corridor: low population density, limited ambient light, predictable traffic patterns, and a natural topography that allows for low-altitude operations without detection by ground-based observation. Survey units have used this corridor on seven previous cycles without incident.

Field Note — Navigation Officer / Cycle 7.441.09 / Pre-Contact

Approach vector nominal. Mountain corridor clear. One Terran ground vehicle detected travelling south at moderate velocity. Occupancy: two subjects. Vehicle velocity and trajectory consistent with long-transit return journey. Assessment: subjects in fatigued state. Window for intercept estimated at [APPROX: 4 minutes] without deviation from survey path. Recommending situational authorisation. No other traffic detected within [APPROX: 18 kilometres].

The decision to intercept was made by the mission lead based on the rarity of a paired Terran contact opportunity in a low-risk corridor. Single subjects are more common; paired subjects — particularly a paired male-female bonded unit — provide superior comparative data on Terran psychological and physiological dimorphism. The authorisation was logged and the intercept sequence initiated.

Intended neurological effect: mild sedation and temporal perception alteration [APPROX: dissociation]. Both subjects experience the expected disorientation response. Vehicle brought to controlled stop. Standard retrieval procedure commenced.

CYCLE 7.441.09 / 22:24 LOCAL

Both subjects aboard vessel. Subjects separated per standard protocol. Ambient environment adjusted to reduce acute stress response: lighting moderated, temperature elevated 2.1 degrees [APPROX], atmosphere composition adjusted to introduce mild sedation compound [APPROX: analogous to Terran benzodiazepine effect but non-pharmacological — pressure-based]. Subject B remains in significantly elevated distress. Subject A's distress level is notably lower than predicted for first-contact scenario. Anomalous.

Observational Note — Lead Examiner

Subject A's composure during initial retrieval was outside expected parameters for a Terran Stage 2.4 specimen. Standard prediction models for this developmental stage anticipate acute panic and non-communicative withdrawal during the first 8–12 [APPROX: minutes] of contact. Subject A was communicative within [APPROX: 90 seconds] of boarding. She initiated verbal contact with crew members unprompted. This was noted immediately and the contact protocol was adjusted accordingly. Subject A was designated primary interaction candidate. This designation held throughout the contact event.

The examination of Subject B produced a finding that, while not medically unusual for this species, was ethically significant. We are cataloguing biological specimens from a species that is, in this developmental period, significantly harming its own members. The survey record should reflect this. It is relevant to any civilisational stage assessment.

Section 05

The Cartographic Incident — Star Map Exchange

The following sequence was not planned and represents the most operationally significant element of the contact event. It is logged here in full.

The Question

During the post-examination interaction with Subject A, she asked directly — verbally, without hesitation — where the survey team was from. This question has been asked in previous Terran contact events, but typically in a state of shock or dissociation, with little expectation of an answer. Subject A asked it calmly and with evident genuine curiosity. She wanted an actual answer.

Interaction Log — Lead Examiner / Subject A Exchange

Subject A: "Where are you from?"

Examiner asked [APPROX]: whether Subject A had any knowledge of stellar cartography or the broader structure of her galactic neighbourhood. Subject A acknowledged she did not — not with embarrassment, but with something closer to an invitation. She was inviting us to show her something she did not know. The examiner made a decision that was not formally within the contact protocol but was not prohibited by it. The navigational reference chart was retrieved from the operational display and shown to Subject A.

The Map

The chart shown to Subject A was a standard operational navigational reference — a three-dimensional stellar display rendered in the flat projection used for briefing purposes aboard Survey Unit Θ-9. It showed the survey vessel's primary operational region: the home binary system and its surrounding stellar neighbourhood within approximately [APPROX: 55 light-years], with route classifications marked in the standard notation: heavy-use routes, standard routes, and exploratory/expeditionary paths.

The examiner indicated the home system — the two central bodies — and gestured to indicate origin. Subject A studied the chart for an extended period. She asked what the different types of lines meant. The examiner explained the route classification system in approximate terms she could process. Subject A continued to study the chart. She did not panic. She did not ask to be returned to her vehicle. She asked questions about the other stars shown. The session extended considerably beyond its intended duration.

The Decision to Show the Map — Internal Record

The decision to show Subject A the navigational reference was unusual within the protocol. The lead examiner's recorded rationale was as follows: Subject A had asked a direct and intelligent question with evident genuine intent. She was not asking in panic or as a displacement behaviour — she was asking because she wanted to know. The examiner considered that the map — a complex three-dimensional stellar reference — would be functionally meaningless to a Stage 2.4 Terran. She would not have the stellar cataloguing knowledge to interpret it without the same chart for reference. The examiner's assessment: the risk of meaningful intelligence transfer was low; the significance of the interaction itself was high.

Lead Examiner's Note — Cartographic Incident / Post-Event Reflection

What the examiner did not adequately anticipate was Subject A's memory retention. The Terran neural architecture — particularly in subjects with elevated prefrontal coherence such as Subject A — is capable of retaining partial stellar pattern configurations even from brief visual exposure, particularly under conditions of heightened attention. Subject A was paying very close attention. She was attempting to memorise the chart. The examiner observed this and did not intervene. This decision will be examined at the next sector review. The examiner's view remains that it was correct.

Sector Command Annotation — Added Post-Review

The examiner's decision is noted without formal censure. The chart shown was a standard operational reference and not an intelligence document of the first order. However, the subsequent events — see Section 07 — indicate that Subject A's retention of the pattern was more precise than the examiner's on-site assessment suggested. This case is the primary reason for the updated contact protocol guidance issued in Cycle 7.441.14, which now specifies that operational display materials should not be accessible during subject interaction sessions. The relevant passage reads: "The cognitive capacity of atypical specimens should not be estimated from species-average parameters."

SUBJECTS RETURNED TO
Terran ground vehicle, road position approximately 35 Terran miles south of intercept point. Consistent with standard repositioning.

The release of Subject A requires a note. She was returned to the vehicle, and the suppression sequence was applied as standard. Subject A did not resist. She had, during the final moments of contact, asked whether she would remember. The examiner told her she would not — and at the conscious level, that remained true. What the examiner did not say, and may not have realised, was that the deeper pattern of her experience — the emotional sequence, the visual impressions, the stellar chart — had been encoded below the threshold that the suppression protocol targets.

Both subjects were repositioned south of the intercept point. The temporal gap between their last conscious memory on approach and their first conscious memory of departure — approximately two hours — was consistent across both subjects. This consistency later became a significant factor in how Terran researchers evaluated their account. We had not intended to leave them with a coherent two-person confirmation of a shared event. We had intended to leave them with nothing at all.

Suppression Unit Assessment — Post-Release

Subject A represents a suppression failure of a kind not previously recorded for this type of protocol at Grade 3. She did not consciously remember, but she dreamed. The dreams began within days. They were not random — they reconstructed, with significant accuracy, the sequence of events aboard the vessel. This mechanism is not well understood. The Terran sleep-state appears to process and partially recover experiences that the suppression sequence specifically targeted for erasure. We do not know how to prevent this in high-coherence specimens. We recommend a review of suppression grade assignments for subjects with elevated prefrontal readings.

Section 07

Post-Event Surveillance Log

Standard post-contact monitoring of both subjects was conducted across the subsequent Terran years. The following is a summary of significant developments.

CYCLE 7.441.09 / DAY 3 POST-EVENT

Subject A composes a written report to a Terran civilian investigative organisation [NICAP — National Investigations Committee on Aerial Phenomena]. The report is accurate, measured, and includes sufficient technical detail to be considered a credible first-person account. Subject A has chosen to report rather than conceal. This is the expected behaviour of a subject with her civic and social profile.

CYCLE 7.441.09 / MONTH 2 POST-EVENT

A Terran field investigator [Designation: Webb, W.] interviews both subjects. His assessment — transmitted internally within NICAP — rates them as highly credible. He notes in particular their emotional composure and the absence of any apparent material motivation for the account. He is not wrong. Monitoring continues.

CYCLE 7.441 / APPROXIMATELY 3 TERRAN YEARS POST-EVENT

Both subjects begin a series of sessions with a Terran psychiatrist and neurologist specialising in hypnotic regression [Designation: Simon, B.]. Sessions are conducted separately. Both subjects recover substantial conscious recall of the contact event under hypnosis. The two accounts are, in their essential details, consistent with each other. They are also consistent with our field record. The Terran hypnotic process has penetrated the suppression at the subcortical level. This is the second confirmed instance of suppression breach in this subject pair. Both are now logged.

CYCLE 7.441 / APPROXIMATELY 3 TERRAN YEARS POST-EVENT — CRITICAL

Under hypnotic regression, Subject A produces a hand-drawn reproduction of the navigational reference chart she was shown during the contact event. The reproduction is partial — twelve of the primary stellar objects are depicted, with the two central bodies identified and the route classifications broadly correct. The accuracy of Subject A's recall significantly exceeded the suppression unit's post-release assessment. The chart is passed to Terran researchers for analysis.

CYCLE 7.442 / APPROXIMATELY 8 TERRAN YEARS POST-EVENT — CRITICAL

A Terran civilian researcher [Designation: Fish, M.] constructs a three-dimensional physical model of stars within [APPROX: 55 light-years] of Terran origin using newly published stellar catalogue data. She tests 26 different viewing angles over a period of approximately 3 Terran years. She identifies a match between Subject A's chart and the survey region as viewed from the correct vantage. The stellar identifications she produces include the home binary system, and several of the route-corridor stars. Her accuracy is approximately 70% of the original chart. A Terran civilian has, without access to any intelligence materials, used publicly available stellar data and Subject A's hand-drawn memory to reconstruct a working approximation of our operational survey region. This is the most significant intelligence event arising from the contact event. It is logged at the highest available classification level.

CYCLE 7.441 / APPROXIMATELY 5 TERRAN YEARS POST-EVENT

Subject B's health continues to deteriorate. The cardiovascular burden flagged in the medical examination log has worsened. He remains professionally active and engaged in the civil rights work that has defined his life. He dies in Cycle 7.441 / Year 8 post-event at [APPROX: 46 Terran years]. This is logged with the notation that his death preceded his ability to see the full account of the contact event enter the public record. He did not know, when he died, the scale of what he and Subject A had contributed to Terran awareness of the phenomenon. The record notes this.

CYCLE 7.442 / ONGOING — SUBJECT A

Subject A continues to engage publicly with the contact event until her death in Cycle 7.443 [APPROX: 2004 Terran calendar]. She never wavers in her account. She is 85 Terran years at death. Her dwelling in Portsmouth, Northern Continent Sector 4, is eventually designated as a site of historical significance by regional Terran authorities. The woman who asked a navigator where he was from, and remembered the answer, is commemorated on a metal sign attached to a building. We note this without further comment.

Intelligence Assessment — Cartographic Breach Summary

The Fish reconstruction of the operational survey chart should be treated as a significant intelligence event regardless of the limited attention it received in mainstream Terran scientific circles at the time. A Terran civilian with no access to classified materials produced an approximately 70% accurate reconstruction of our sector survey region using: (1) public stellar cataloguing data, and (2) a hand-drawn chart produced from memory by a subject who should not have retained that memory. The combination of Subject A's neurological capacity and this researcher's methodological persistence achieved something our suppression protocols were explicitly designed to prevent. Both individuals are to be noted in the sector's historical record.

Assessor's Note — Civilisational Readiness

The low readiness-for-formal-contact rating does not reflect any deficiency in Terran individual cognitive capacity — as this very contact event demonstrates, individual specimens can be remarkably capable. It reflects the structural state of the civilisation as a whole: a species in possession of weapons sufficient for self-annihilation, still distributing resources and rights unequally among its own members, still organised into competitive territorial blocs, still causing significant suffering within its own population. These are Stage 2.4 indicators. They are not permanent. They are a phase. But formal contact during this phase carries risk — not to us, but to them.

Closing Assessment — Lead Examiner / Final Entry

This species is not ready. That is the formal assessment, and it is accurate. But readiness is not the only thing worth noting in a field record. What is also worth noting is this: a woman sat in a room aboard our vessel in the mountains of a small planet and was not afraid of us. She asked where we were from. She looked at a chart that mapped a section of the galaxy she had never seen and tried, carefully and deliberately, to memorise it. Decades later, a second woman on the same planet found her drawing and — using nothing but a catalogue of nearby stars and three years of patient work — located us.

The civilisation is not ready. Some of its members are extraordinary. These two facts are not in contradiction. They are, in fact, what makes this one worth watching.
